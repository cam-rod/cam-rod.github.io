About me

A computer engineering student.

I got my coding start in high school, with a somewhat brutal introduction to coding via Java. Luckily, the next year we used Python, and I was hooked. I also got to use a Raspberry Pi, kicking off my interest in hardware as well. I started my bachelor's at the University of Toronto, picking up C and C++ in software courses, and discovered Verilog and FPGAs (one of UofT's strengths) in hardware. Eventually I took these skills to my co-ops, a research position working on compression algorithms for FPGAs, and a year-long internship working on security at Red Hat.

All the while, my interest kept me coding outside the classroom. I started off small: a (somewhat broken) physics program for the TI-84, a patch for a Reddit moderation bot. Then came my first big project: XKCDAltTextBot, a script that brought the follow-up joke of the popular webcomic to Twitter. I dove into the challenge, even refusing to use Tweepy so that I could learn the API calls myself. I'd use this project in my university applications, and continued to update it until Heroku shut down the free tier, and Twitter siloed itself.

Since then, I've continued to experiment. I joined UTAT and wrote software for microcontrollers, eventually becoming the Firmware system lead. I opened numerous PRs after gaining experience at Red Hat, and even got my RHCSA certification. Of course, I kept learning new things like Rust, GraphQL, and JavaScript as I went.

A nerd.

Complete shocker. I follow new technology, and experiment with unique or interesting stuff. Whether it's trying to create a breadboard circuit, or patching a bug myself, I prefer to get hands-on. I also like to discover how gadgets work, and sometimes that means taking apart and putting it back together well enough. Oddly enough, I'm half-decent at writing, which is why this is the 4th paragraph on the page.
